In-House IT vs. Outsourced Tech Support: A Real Cost Breakdown

What hiring actually costs — and what you get when you outsource instead.

📅 February 2026 ⏱ 7 min read ✍️ Tech Chimps Team

At some point, most growing businesses face the same question: we need better tech support — do we hire someone, or do we outsource it?

The instinct is often to hire. Having someone on-site feels more reliable, more controllable, more "real." But the numbers tell a different story. Here's a clear-eyed breakdown of what both options actually cost — and what you actually get.

The True Cost of an In-House IT Hire in Canada

Let's use Toronto as the benchmark. An IT Support Specialist in the GTA earns between $55,000 and $75,000 in base salary. But salary is never the full picture.

  • Base salary: $60,000–$75,000
  • Employer CPP & EI contributions: ~$4,500
  • Benefits (health, dental, vision): $4,000–$8,000
  • Vacation pay (3 weeks standard): ~$3,500
  • Hardware, software licences, desk: $3,000–$5,000 (year one)
  • Recruiting fees (if using agency): $8,000–$15,000 one-time
  • Training and onboarding time: 4–8 weeks of reduced productivity

Year one total: $83,000–$110,000+. And that's before you account for the risk of a bad hire, staff turnover, or the weeks your business runs without support during a vacancy.

What You're Actually Paying For — and What You're Not Getting

An in-house IT hire typically covers: password resets, laptop setup and troubleshooting, basic network issues, and software installations. That's it. They won't be experts in your CRM, your billing platform, your telephony system, and your cloud infrastructure simultaneously. And they definitely won't cover evenings, weekends, or holidays — unless you pay extra for that.

Most small business IT hires spend 60% of their time on routine ticket-level work that any well-run help desk team can handle remotely for a fraction of the cost.

The Cost of Outsourced Tech Support

Outsourced tech support pricing varies by provider and scope. At Tech Chimps, support packages start from $500/month for smaller businesses and scale based on hours and coverage needs.

For a comparable level of support to a full-time hire — covering help desk, ticket management, user onboarding, and proactive troubleshooting during business hours — a Growth-tier outsourced arrangement typically runs $1,400–$2,000/month. That's $16,800–$24,000 annually versus $83,000–$110,000 for the equivalent in-house role.

The math is stark. You're looking at 75–80% cost savings for the same or better coverage.

What Outsourcing Gives You That In-House Doesn't

  • No vacancy risk. When your in-house IT person quits, you lose weeks of coverage. With an outsourced team, there's no gap.
  • Broader skill coverage. A team of specialists can handle more than one generalist can. You get access to expertise in Microsoft 365, network issues, CRM support, and more — without hiring five different people.
  • Scalable hours. Need more support during a product launch? Scale up. Slower season? Scale down. You can't do that with a salary.
  • Faster response during incidents. A well-run help desk has documented escalation paths. An overwhelmed one-person IT department usually doesn't.

When In-House Still Makes Sense

To be fair: there are situations where in-house IT is the right call. If you have 200+ employees on a single site with complex on-premise infrastructure, a dedicated IT person (or team) likely makes sense. If your business has regulatory requirements for on-site IT oversight, same story.

But for businesses under 100 employees operating on cloud-based tools? The economics of outsourcing almost always win.

Questions to Ask Before You Decide

  • How many support tickets does your team generate per week?
  • What percentage of those are routine (password resets, software issues) vs. complex infrastructure problems?
  • Do you need after-hours coverage?
  • Can you afford 4–12 weeks without support if your IT hire leaves?
